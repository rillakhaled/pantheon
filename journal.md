For someone whose music is unashamedly and joyously sexual in nature and content, it begs the question: how does one embrace that (in our cultural context) in one’s identity and exude it as energy during performance without it becoming uncomfortable for others?  
  
I can’t speak for everyone, so let me rephrase this: why am I not made uncomfortable by Peaches being very sexual nearby?  
  
I think it’s to do with finding a negotiation between: Peaches the phenomenon && concert performance context && receptivity of audience.  
  
By finding that sweet spot in which those above elements are translating each other, all of them are now using each other’s language.  
  
They are communicating with each other and they have chemistry.  
  
We can tell because we perceive chemistry at a sensory level.  
  
At a crude level this might be a way to establish whether a creative work “works”: do its components have chemistry with each other.  
  
Another observation: Peaches commits hard. She does not hold back. This is what stops her work feeling like parody. From a reality layering perspective, for the duration of that performance, that is who Peaches is and her sheer commitment to it is what gives me an invitation to appreciate it wholesomely.  
  
This led to a chain of thought:  
  
I need Pantheon’s conceptual components to have chemistry: they need to genuinely converse and meet each other in a middle space in which they are understood by each other.  
  
Each of the conceptual components should not be holding back:  
  

- the e-commerce thing needs to fully be the e-commerce thing
- the gods are here thing needs to fully be the gods are here thing

  
For the chemistry/conversation, the conceptual components need to translate to each other, in a way that allows both of them to remain faithful to their regular logics.  
  
How do I know if I’m avoiding the zone of, “this is super offensive”?  
  
If I am willing to genuinely agree that the hybrid proposition makes sense in its shared terms and shared logics.  
  
So the guiding values are:  

- mutual shared “authenticity”
- fictional internal consistency
- at the diegetic level, I ought to find the concept reasonable
  
The UX of comparison, selection, and customization -- the backbone of online retail experience -- should be clearly foregrounded.  
  
I want to steer clear of attaching any price to gods, but I want to retain the weird catharsis of hitting a “pay now” button (please let me not be the only one who experiences this. For a variety of reasons.)  
  
My “pay now” equivalent should trigger a feeling of handing something over that is genuinely meaningful to me in exchange for something else.  
  
- what am I giving?  
- what am I receiving?  
  
Now: on what motivates and justifies praying to a god and adhering to a religion. There are multiple completely pragmatic and secular reasons for adhering to a religion that I personally identify with most, but they’re too dry and in the wrong spirit (perhaps because they’re aspiritual).  
  
Let’s go spiritually big. Let’s go infinite gain.  
  
Assuming Pascal’s Wager logic, most folks who identify with a religion that has some kind of heaven have a claim to being able to experience that heaven (let’s call them heaven-likes) in exchange for belief in the religion/prayer to its god(s). Importantly, these heaven-likes are desirable because they have some aspect of “infinite awesome”.  
  
Let's go spiritually small. Let's go finite gain.  
  
Folks who willingly believe & practice a religion do so because they are getting something spiritually positive out of it during their lifetimes.  
  
So here is the concept:  
  
If my cost of entry into a heaven-like is regular belief & practice, does that belief & practice have to come strictly from me, or can I contract it out to other people?  
  
Assuming I can contract my belief & practices to others, does that mean I can borrow the power of their gods temporarily in exchange for picking up their beliefs & practices temporarily?  
  
We would maintain our collective rights to our heaven-likes because the same amount of belief & practice would be happening.  
  
What am I giving?  

- Some amount of the spiritually positive “during my lifetime” gains I receive from my base religion
- Some of the requirement to believe and practice my base religion
  
What am I receiving?

- Some amount of someone else’s spiritually positive “during their lifetime” gains from their base religion
- A temporary requirement to believe and practice in another religion

Now let's add network effects. Like timesharing, I can "buy" my way into a pool of customers by spending a relatively small amount, but our collective capital is enough for us to collectively own something. 

I can only access the benefits of a particular religion if someone in my pool has it as a base religion. So I'm incentivized to make it a rich pool.
